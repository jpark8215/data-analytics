How a Client Saved $200K by Asking a Better Question

How reframing their search within data they already had completely changed their business trajectory.

Six months ago, a client's logistics team was staring at a burning $200,000 problem. Due to what they thought were unpredictable supply chain delays, expedite fees for their premium product lines were skyrocketing. If they missed shipping deadlines for these VIP clients, they faced contract penalties. The knee-jerk reaction? Spend resources heavily to investigate global shipping lane delays.

They hired an external consultancy and pulled months of port delay records, weather patterns, and supplier lead times to build a massive dashboard predicting "when delays would happen."

The result? A beautifully complex model that did absolutely nothing to solve their problem. The delays were macro-level issues out of their control. Knowing a delay was happening didn't stop them from having to pay the $200K in expedite fees to overcome it.

"They were looking at the right problem but asking the entirely wrong question of their data."

The Pivot: Looking Inward, Not Outward

Frustrated, they brought the problem back to their internal data team. Instead of asking, "Why are shipments delayed?", one analyst reframed the entire investigation.

She asked: "Do these delayed VIP orders actually *need* to go out on rushed shipping to avoid penalties?"

This shifted their focus from macro-level port delays to their own internal CRM and customer contract data, data they had possessed the entire time but had completely ignored in this context.

The Discovery They Missed

It turned out the data they needed wasn't in global shipping APIs; it was sitting quietly in their Salesforce instance.

By analyzing the Service Level Agreements (SLAs) of the VIP clients, they found a fascinating discrepancy. The system had a hardcoded rule to default all VIP orders to "Overnight Air" if they fell behind schedule. But their data showed that 70% of those specific VIP contracts allowed for a flexible 5-day delivery window if notified in advance.

Before (The Wrong Question)

Question: How can we predict global supply chain delays to warn the logistics team?

Action: Building complex predictive models on external data.

Result: Realized delays were unavoidable. Paid $200K in overnight shipping fees anyway.

After (The Right Question)

Question: Which delayed VIP orders actually legally require expedited shipping?

Action: Merged shipping pipeline data with contract SLA data in CRM.

Result: Switched 70% of delayed orders to standard ground shipping. Saved the $200K entirely.

The Takeaway: Question Your Questions

Organizations often fall into the trap of believing that new problems require new data. We assume that if we just buy another dataset, API, or dashboard, the answer will reveal itself.

But more often than not, the solution lies in recombining the data you already have to answer a more targeted, actionable question. The next time you face a costly operational issue, before you buy new data, ask yourself:

  • Are you treating a symptom (unavoidable delays) or investigating the actual cost driver (your policy reaction)?
  • What internal data do you have that defines the rules of your reaction?

Sometimes, saving $200K isn't about outsmarting the global supply chain. It's about realizing your own system is rushing packages that nobody asked to be rushed.
