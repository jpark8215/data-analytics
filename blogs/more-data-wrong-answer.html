Why "We Need More Data" Is Sometimes the Wrong Answer

It sounds responsible. It can be a costly delay tactic. Here's how to tell the difference.

Picture a meeting where a decision needs to be made to launch a new product line, enter a new market, restructure a team. The spreadsheets are open, the dashboards are loaded, and someone around the table says the four words that kill momentum in organizations everywhere:

"We need more data."

It sounds responsible. It sounds scientific. It sounds like the mature, analytically rigorous thing to say. And sometimes genuinely, it is the right answer. But often, it isn't. Often, it is an expensive delay dressed up as diligence. And the inability to tell the difference is costing organizations real money, real time, and real competitive ground.

This post is for the non-data person in that meeting, the manager, the executive, the department head who needs a clear framework for pushing back on that phrase without sounding reckless.

The Concept Nobody Explains: Diminishing Returns

In economics, "diminishing returns" describes a principle that is intuitive once you see it: the more you add of something, the less each additional unit contributes. The first cup of coffee in the morning does a lot. The fourth cup does very little. The tenth cup is counterproductive.

Data works exactly the same way. The first data you collect on a problem a few hundred customer surveys, a week of sales figures, a small pilot study dramatically improves your understanding. That first batch of evidence moves you from guessing to informed. It is immensely valuable.

But what happens when you collect more? The hundredth data point adds less than the tenth. The thousandth adds less than the hundredth. At some point, additional data barely moves the needle on your understanding at all yet it continues to cost time, money, and organizational attention.

"The question is never 'do we have all possible data?' The right question is: 'does additional data meaningfully change what we would do?' If the answer is no, you already have enough."

This is not a theoretical observation. It is backed by decades of research in decision science and information economics.

60–70%
of a decision's informational value comes from the first 20% of available data, across multiple empirical studies (Hogarth & Karelaia, 2007)

average improvement in decision quality from doubling sample size once a threshold is reached (Gigerenzen et al., Simple Heuristics, 1999)

The Parable of the Weather Forecast

Here is an analogy that makes this concrete for any audience. Imagine you need to decide whether to hold your company's outdoor event next Saturday. You check the weather forecast on Monday. It says 70% chance of rain. You check again Tuesday. 68% chance of rain. Wednesday: 72%. Thursday: 71%. Friday: 73%.

Each new forecast gave you slightly different information, but the fundamental picture, it will probably rain, never changed. Checking the forecast six more times would not have helped you decide any better. The decision move the event indoors was warranted by Monday's data. Every day you delayed was a day lost for logistical planning.

The Principle in Plain English When additional information keeps pointing in the same direction as what you already have, it is confirming a signal, not adding one. Confirmation has value but that value decreases rapidly and must be weighed against the cost of waiting.

The Hidden Cost That Nobody Puts on the Spreadsheet

When someone asks for more data, we naturally think about the cost of gathering it: analyst hours, survey costs, research budgets. These are real, but they are rarely the biggest cost. The biggest cost is almost always the opportunity cost of the delayed decision itself.

Consider these three scenarios, all drawn from patterns documented in business literature:

The product launch window. A consumer goods company ran focus groups for six months on a new product concept. The data at month two was directionally identical to the data at month six customers liked the concept, with caveats about price. The additional four months of research cost the company its first-mover advantage. A competitor launched a similar product at month four. Research by Peter Golder and Gerard Tellis (1993, Journal of Marketing Research) found that market pioneers capture an average of 47% higher market share than late followers, even when the follower's product is equivalent.

The hiring decision. Research by University of Oregon psychologist Paul Meehl replicated dozens of times since his foundational 1954 study showed that simple statistical models outperformed clinical expert judgment in predicting outcomes in areas from parole recidivism to academic performance. His core finding: adding more unstructured data (more interviews, more reference checks beyond a certain point) actively degraded prediction accuracy because it introduced noise and cognitive bias, not signal. At some point, more information makes humans worse decision-makers, not better.

The crisis response. In high-stakes, time-sensitive scenarios, the cost of waiting for more data is not just opportunity cost. It can be catastrophic. Studies of military command decision-making (Klein, Sources of Power, 1998) found that expert commanders rarely waited for complete information. They acted on the first scenario that seemed "good enough" what Klein called "recognition-primed decision-making" and course-corrected in real time. Waiting for perfect data in a dynamic environment often means that by the time you decide, the situation has changed and your data is already stale.

Decision Urgency: The Variable Everyone Ignores

How much data is "enough" is not a fixed threshold. It is always a function of how urgent the decision is. The same amount of data that is insufficient for a ten-year infrastructure investment may be wildly over-sufficient for a quarterly marketing allocation. Yet most organizations apply the same default standard "we need more" regardless of stakes or timeline.

A simple mental model: every decision exists somewhere on two axes.

✓ When you should get more data

  • The decision is largely irreversible (a large acquisition, major capital expenditure)
  • Current data points in genuinely conflicting directions
  • You can get the missing data quickly and cheaply
  • The downside of a wrong decision vastly outweighs the cost of delay

✗ When "more data" is the wrong call

  • The decision is reversible, you can change course if needed
  • Existing data already points consistently in one direction
  • Gathering more data will take longer than the decision window
  • The real blocker is disagreement, not information

That last point deserves special attention: sometimes "we need more data" is a way of avoiding a conversation about disagreement. Two executives don't agree on a strategic direction. Rather than surface and resolve the conflict, someone calls for another round of research. The data comes back. It supports both positions, depending on how you frame it. Nothing is resolved. Another study is commissioned. This pattern is sometimes called "analysis paralysis," but a more precise diagnosis is conflict avoidance disguised as rigor.

A useful diagnostic: ask "What specific new information would change our decision?" If nobody can answer that clearly, the problem is not data. The problem is alignment.

What Research Actually Says About "Enough" Data

The academic study of how much information is optimal for decisions has a long history. A few findings worth knowing:

The "less is more" effect. German psychologist Gerd Gigerenzen and colleagues have spent decades documenting cases where simple heuristics, rules of thumb using minimal data, outperform complex statistical models fed with rich data. Their "Take the Best" heuristic, which picks a decision based on the single most predictive cue rather than combining all available cues, matched or beat multiple regression in 19 out of 20 real-world datasets they tested (Gigerenzen & Goldstein, Psychological Review, 1996). More data, fed through more complex models, can actually reduce accuracy when the signal-to-noise ratio is low.

The confidence–accuracy gap. A well-replicated finding in cognitive psychology is that as people receive more information, their confidence in their judgment rises faster than their actual accuracy. In a landmark 1973 study by Paul Slovic, professional handicappers evaluating horse races showed steadily increasing confidence as they were given more variables about each horse but their predictive accuracy plateaued after the first 5 variables. Additional information made them feel better about their decisions, not actually make better ones. This is the practical danger: more data satisfies the feeling of due diligence while delivering diminishing real returns.

Sequential versus batch decisions. Research in Bayesian decision theory distinguishes between decisions where you can learn and adjust (sequential) versus one-time, all-or-nothing decisions. For sequential decisions rolling out a new pricing model region by region, testing a marketing message with a small group before scaling less initial data is optimal, because you will get feedback and course-correct. Demanding a complete dataset before any action collapses an inherently sequential process into a falsely binary one.

The Amazon "Two-Pizza" Framework: Build In Reversibility

One of the most widely cited frameworks for managing decision data requirements comes from Amazon's internal operating principles. Amazon categorizes decisions into two types:

Type 1 decisions are irreversible, high-stakes, and consequential, like entering a new business category or acquiring a company. These deserve extensive data gathering, deliberation, and scrutiny. The cost of being wrong is high and hard to undo.

Type 2 decisions are reversible, lower-stakes, and recoverable, like changing a feature on a website, adjusting a marketing channel mix, or experimenting with a new team structure. These should be made quickly with available data and corrected if the outcome is poor. Jeff Bezos has noted publicly that most decisions in most organizations are Type 2, but are treated with the process overhead of Type 1, which is precisely how organizations become slow, risk-averse, and analytically paralyzed.

Practical Test Before calling for more data, ask yourself: "If we decide now and we're wrong, what happens? Can we fix it?" If the answer is "yes, within a reasonable cost," that is a Type 2 decision. Act on what you have. If the answer is "no, this commits us for years," treat it like Type 1 and gather more data deliberately and purposefully.

How to Actually Know When You Have Enough

There is no universal formula, but there are three practical tests that work for most non-technical decision-makers:

The "flip test." Ask: "Is there any realistic new data point that would flip our current direction?" Walk through what that data would have to show. If it is extraordinarily unlikely, or if no one can articulate it, you have enough. The decision has already been made by the data you have, you are just not admitting it yet.

The "cost of error" test. Estimate, even roughly, the cost of being wrong vs. the cost of waiting. If you're choosing between a $50,000 marketing spend and losing six weeks to gather better audience data, calculate what six weeks of lost lead generation actually costs. Many organizations find that the cost of additional research regularly exceeds the cost of a wrong decision that could be corrected in the next cycle.

The "what would change" test. Ask every person in the room to write down, before the discussion: "What specific finding from additional research would change my recommendation?" If the answers are vague (more confidence, a clearer picture) rather than specific (if churn exceeds 15% in the first 90 days), the issue is not data, it is uncertainty tolerance. That is a leadership conversation, not an analytics one.

The Culture Problem Underneath

It is worth naming something that data people know but rarely say out loud in executive meetings: in many organizations, asking for more data is not primarily about the data. It is about accountability avoidance. If a decision goes wrong and it was made with "incomplete data," the decision-maker can shift blame to the data gap. If it was made with extensive analysis, the blame can be shifted to the analysts. The request for more data is sometimes a sophisticated form of risk transfer.

A 2021 survey by NewVantage Partners found that 92% of Fortune 500 data and AI leaders identified "cultural resistance to data-driven change" as their primary obstacle, not technology or talent. Part of that resistance is an unwillingness to own decisions clearly enough to commit to them on available evidence.

Organizations that make the best use of data are not those that demand the most data before every decision. They are the ones that create genuine accountability for decisions, which makes leaders more motivated to make high-quality ones quickly, rather than endlessly deferred ones that are immune to accountability.

"The goal of data is not to eliminate accountability, it is to sharpen it. A decision made with clear, sufficient data and owned by a named decision-maker is a far healthier organizational artifact than a committee conclusion reached after six months of inconclusive research."

The Bottom Line, in Plain English

Data collection has a point of diminishing returns. That point arrives earlier than most people think, and the cost of crossing past it, in time, money, and momentum, is almost always underestimated.

"We need more data" is the right answer when the missing information is specific, obtainable in time, and genuinely likely to change your direction. It is the wrong answer when the data you have already points clearly, when the cost of delay exceeds the cost of a correctable mistake, or when the real problem is not information but alignment, courage, or accountability.

The next time you hear those four words in a meeting, ask one question: "What specific data, if we had it, would change what we recommend?"

If the answer is crisp, go get it. If the answer is vague, you already have enough.
