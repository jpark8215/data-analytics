The 5-Step Framework: Messy Problem to Clean Data Question

From a vague executive mandate to a measurable, actionable analytical strategy.

One of the most common reasons data projects fail isn't bad machine learning models or missing data, it's that the team solved the wrong problem. Business leaders often approach data teams with vague frustrations disguised as requests. To build something useful, you have to bridge the gap and translate everyday business anxiety into measurable, logical data questions.

Over the years, I've developed a reliable 5-step framework to clarify these situations. To demonstrate how it works, let’s walk through a critical real-world problem derived from the US healthcare system.

The Messy Business Problem

"Our hospital is bleeding money because of Medicare readmission penalties. We need to stop patients from coming back so much!"

Step 1: Unpack the Core Objective

We first need to filter emotion from facts. The hospital isn't just trying to "stop patients from coming back"—reducing all readmissions indiscriminately might mean turning away patients who genuinely need critical care, raising mortality rates and liability.

The refined objective: Reduce preventable 30-day readmissions for Medicare advantage patients to minimize financial penalties without compromising patient health.

Step 2: Identify the Actors and Actions

Who is involved, and what can they actually do? If your models predict a patient will return, what intervention occurs?

In our healthcare example, the actors are the discharge nurses and case managers. The actions they can take include scheduling follow-up calls within 48 hours, arranging transportation to outpatient clinics, or prescribing an at-home monitoring kit.

Step 3: Define the Measurable Outcomes (The "Y")

If we want to reduce readmissions, we need a concrete target variable. In data terms, what exactly are we predicting or classifying?

The outcome definition: A binary target—1 if a discharged high-risk patient is readmitted to any hospital facility within 30 days of their initial discharge date, and 0 otherwise.

Step 4: Map the Available Information (The "X")

What data is available at the exact moment the decision needs to be made? It doesn't help to use billing summaries that won't finalize until two weeks after the patient goes home.

At the time of discharge, the care team only has access to: EHR admission notes, patient demographics, historical chronic conditions, vital sign trends during the stay, and the patient's zip code (for proxying social determinants of health).

Step 5: Formulate the Final Data Question

Combine all previous steps into a single, highly specific technical statement that serves as the guiding star for the entire data project.

The Clean Data Question

"Using historical EHR admitting logs, past diagnosis codes, and vital sign trends available at the time of discharge, can we predict the probability (0-100%) that a Medicare patient will be readmitted within 30 days, so that case managers can prioritize scheduling 48-hour follow-up calls for the top 15% highest-risk patients?"

By moving from "stop patients from coming back" to a hyper-specific, actor-driven prediction bound by exact time constraints, you provide your data engineers, analysts, and scientists with a clear, achievable target that guarantees business value.
